Part A: Understanding the CIA Triad


1. Confidentiality

Definition:
Confidentiality means keeping information private and ensuring only authorized people can access it.

Why it is important:
It protects sensitive data such as passwords, personal information, bank details, and company secrets from being exposed to unauthorized users or hackers.

Real-world example:
When you log into your email or banking app, your data is protected using passwords and encryption so that only you can access your account.

2. Integrity

Definition:
Integrity means ensuring that data is accurate, complete, and has not been altered or tampered with.

Why it is important:
It ensures that information remains trustworthy. If data is changed without permission, it can lead to wrong decisions or security risks.

Real-world example:
When you transfer money online, the system ensures the amount is not changed during transmission.

3. Availability

Definition:
Availability means that information and systems are accessible to authorized users whenever they need them.

Why it is important:
If systems go down or data is unavailable, users cannot complete important tasks, which can affect businesses and services.

Real-world example:
A hospital system must always be available so doctors can access patient records in emergencies.

| Concept         | Definition                                      | Importance                               | Real-world Example                           |
| --------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Confidentiality | Protects information from unauthorized access   | Prevents data leaks and privacy breaches | Online banking login protected by password   |
| Integrity       | Ensures data is accurate and not modified       | Maintains trust and correctness of data  | Secure online money transfer without changes |
| Availability    | Ensures systems/data are accessible when needed | Allows continuous access to services     | Hospital systems available for emergency use |


Part B: Real-World Case Study

1. What happened?

The WannaCry ransomware attack happened in May 2017. It was a global cyberattack where hackers used malicious software (ransomware) to infect computers running Windows operating systems. The malware spread very quickly across networks by exploiting a security weakness in Windows. Once infected, the computers were locked, and users were shown a message demanding payment in Bitcoin to unlock their files.

2. Which part of the CIA Triad was affected?

The main part of the CIA Triad affected was:

Availability → Systems and files became inaccessible because they were locked by ransomware.
Integrity (secondary impact) → Some systems were also at risk of data corruption or unauthorized changes.

3. What was the impact?
Many hospitals, businesses, and government systems were shut down or disrupted.
The UK’s National Health Service (NHS) had to cancel appointments and operations.
Over 200,000 computers in 150+ countries were affected.
Companies faced financial losses and downtime.
Important services were delayed, affecting patients and customers.

4. How could it have been prevented?
Regular software updates and patching (Microsoft had already released a fix, but many systems were not updated).
Strong cybersecurity awareness in organizations.
Using antivirus and endpoint protection tools.
Regular data backups so systems could be restored without paying ransom.
Network segmentation to stop the malware from spreading so quickly.

Part D: Threat Identification
1. Insider Threat

Description:
An insider threat happens when someone inside an organization (employee, student, or staff) misuses their access to harm systems or steal data.

Possible Impact:

Data leaks (student records, exams)
Damage to systems or reputation
Unauthorized access to sensitive information

Prevention Methods:

Limit user access (least privilege principle)
Monitor user activity
Use access logs and audits
Strong security policies and training
2. Malware

Description:
Malware is malicious software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to systems (e.g., viruses, worms, ransomware).

Possible Impact:

Data loss or encryption (ransomware)
System slowdown or crash
Theft of personal or financial data

Prevention Methods:

Install antivirus/anti-malware software
Avoid unknown downloads and links
Keep systems updated
Use firewalls
3. Phishing

Description:
Phishing is a cyber attack where attackers trick users into giving sensitive information like passwords or bank details through fake emails or websites.

Possible Impact:

Account hacking
Financial fraud
Identity theft

Prevention Methods:

Do not click suspicious links
Verify sender emails carefully
Use multi-factor authentication (MFA)
Security awareness training
4. Weak Passwords

Description:
Weak passwords are easy-to-guess passwords like “123456” or “password,” which attackers can easily crack.

Possible Impact:

Unauthorized account access
Data theft or misuse
Account takeover

Prevention Methods:

Use strong passwords (mix of letters, numbers, symbols)
Avoid reusing passwords
Use password managers
Enable MFA
5. Unpatched Systems

Description:
Unpatched systems are software or operating systems that have not been updated with the latest security fixes.

Possible Impact:

Hackers exploit known vulnerabilities
Malware infections
System compromise or shutdown

Prevention Methods:

Regular software updates
Enable automatic updates
Patch management system in organizations
Regular security audits

Part E: Security Recommendations


1. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Why it is important:
MFA adds an extra layer of security beyond passwords. Even if a password is stolen, attackers cannot access the account without a second verification step (like OTP).

2. Regular System Updates

Why it is important:
Updates fix security vulnerabilities. Without updates, hackers can exploit known weaknesses in systems and software.

3. Strong Password Policy

Why it is important:
Strong passwords reduce the risk of brute-force attacks and unauthorized access. It ensures users do not use weak or guessable passwords.

4. Employee and Student Security Awareness Training

Why it is important:
Humans are often the weakest link. Training helps users recognize phishing emails, scams, and unsafe behavior.

5. Regular Data Backups

Why it is important:
Backups ensure that important data (student records, exams) can be restored in case of ransomware attacks or system failure.

6. Use Antivirus and Anti-Malware Software

Why it is important:
These tools detect and remove harmful software before it can damage systems or steal data.

7. Access Control (Least Privilege Principle)

Why it is important:
Users should only access data they need. This reduces the risk of insider threats and accidental data leaks.

8. Network Firewalls and Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)

Why it is important:
Firewalls block unauthorized access, while IDS monitors suspicious activity and alerts administrators.

9. Secure Wi-Fi Network (Encrypted Connection)

Why it is important:
Unsecured Wi-Fi can be easily hacked. Encryption (WPA3/WPA2) protects data from being intercepted.

10. Regular Security Audits and Monitoring

Why it is important:
Audits help identify weaknesses in systems before attackers can exploit them. Continuous monitoring detects unusual activity early.
